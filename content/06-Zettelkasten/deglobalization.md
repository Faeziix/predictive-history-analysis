# Deglobalization

**Core idea:** The unwinding of the global free-trade system that defined the post-Cold War era. Not a recession within globalization, but the end of globalization itself.

## The Process

Deglobalization isn't a single event but a structural shift:

1. **Trade weaponization.** Tariffs, sanctions, and export controls replace free trade agreements. Trump's tariffs (largest since 1993) are a leading indicator, not an aberration.

2. **Supply chain retreat.** Firms shift from efficiency (cheapest global source) to security (domestic/allied sources). "Just in time" becomes "just in case."

3. **Financial fragmentation.** SWIFT alternatives (CIPS, SPFS), bilateral currency agreements, de-dollarization. The [[petrodollar-system]] unravels.

4. **Bloc formation.** The world organizes into competing mercantile blocs:
   - [[technate]] (North America)
   - [[pax-judaica-concept]] (Israel-centered Middle East)
   - [[third-rome-concept]] (Russia-centered Eurasia)
   - China-centered Asia-Pacific

5. **Self-sufficiency imperative.** Each bloc must produce food, energy, and manufactured goods domestically. Dependency on external blocs becomes an existential vulnerability.

## Why It's Structural

The WEF itself describes the emerging system as a "multi-nodal trade patchwork" - which is essentially [[CLAIM-012-regional-mercantilism|regional mercantilism]] with polite language.

The drivers are irreversible:
- The [[strait-of-hormuz-chokepoint]] closure demonstrates vulnerability of global trade routes
- Political trust between blocs has been destroyed (sanctions, asset freezes, trade wars)
- Each major power now sees dependency as weakness, not efficiency
- The [[petrodollar-system]] - globalization's financial backbone - is breaking
